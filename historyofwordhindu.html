History of the word 'Hindu'

Hindu as a term is a matter of a lot of speculation.

Some say it’s origin is Arabic and comes from the term Al Hind. Others argue it is a mispronounced term given by the Persians relative to the Sindhu river. Here we try to show multiple texts that have used the term Hindu that appear to have originated in our Holy text itself.

The word Hindu was used extensively long before the advent of the monotheistic religions prevelant today in the context of gentle, beautiful, amiable, adorned, rightful and vanquisher of enemies. When Alexander of Macedonia led an expedition up to Punjab on the country’s western borders, he had the desire to visit the Hindkoot Parvat or Hindukush. The religious scripture of Zoroastrians called 'Shaatir' and their Holy Book 'Avesta' (written hundreds of years before Alexander) also mentions the word Hindu.

According to the Rigveda, ‘Sa’ and ‘Ha’ are one and the same thing. Examined from this perspective, Bhavishya Puran variously refers to the country as Sindhusthan, Hindusthan or Hindustan. In Kalka Purana, ‘Hindavah’ word is used. In Aashvamedhik Parva of Mahabharat, ‘Aryavarta’ is called Hindustan or Sindhustan. Also, as per Briddhasmriti, a Hindu is one who is virtuous, shuns violence but is simultaneously capable of destroying anarchic elements, and protector of the Vedas and cattle. Furthermore, the word Hindu is also used in the Rigveda with ‘hi’ and ‘ndu’ both being used in the context of cow-worshippers. The word Hindu was used in the period of the Rigveda for a cow-worshipper. In Atharva Veda, it is used as it is.

Hence clearing any confusion regarding the term HINDU.
